# 🏛️ Legacy Code: A History of Computing (Volume 1)

> **"The history of computing is, in the main, a grand chronicle of exceptionally clever people solving problems that they had, for the most part, created themselves."**

Most of the load-bearing infrastructure of modern civilisation consists of "Thursday problems"—catastrophic headaches born from "splendidly efficient" Tuesday solutions. This volume is a journey back to the bedrock of digital masonry.

---

## 📖 About the Book
From the days when "programming" required a physical workout to the moment we sent humanity to the Moon on four kilobytes of RAM, **Legacy Code** explores the brilliant, often chaotic origins of the code that still runs our world. 

It is for the veteran engineer who seeks the "why" behind the "how," the student who wonders why their code behaves like a Victorian steam engine, and the curious layperson who suspects that our digital world is held together by bits of string and collective optimism. (Spoiler: It is.)

### 📍 Inside Volume One:
* **The Von Neumann Architecture:** How a stroke of genius in 1945 gave us modern computers and buffer overflows.
* **The Tyranny of the Card:** Why a piece of cardboard from 1928 still dictates your terminal width today.
* **Assembly & Negotiation:** The first attempts to speak to machines without pure binary madness.
* **Grace Hopper & the Literal Bug:** The story of the Admiral and the moth that became a legend.
* **Fortran & COBOL:** Rocket trajectories versus the invisible kings of capitalism.
* **LISP & AI Hype:** A tale of endless parentheses and 60-year cycles.
* **The $5 Billion Gamble:** How IBM’s System/360 invented "backward compatibility."
* **Margaret Hamilton & The Moon:** Coining "Software Engineering" to save astronauts from themselves.
* **The 1968 Crisis:** The moment the industry admitted it had no idea what it was doing.
* **Unix & C:** The OS and language born from a desire to play space travel games.
* **The Relational Revolution:** When Edgar Codd’s math met Larry Ellison’s salesmanship.
* **The First "Lo":** The humble, crashed beginnings of the global internet.
